# Lead Buyer's Playbook - Editing Specification

**Target:** 2,000 words per chapter (±100 words acceptable)  
**Total Reduction:** ~55-60% from current draft  
**Vertical Focus:** B2C consumer direct only (mortgage, insurance, solar)  
**Reader Assumption:** Smart professionals who retain information from previous chapters

---

## Core Narrative Structure

### Sarah's Character Arc

Sarah is the illustrative, aspirational protagonist who mirrors the reader's journey:
- **Starting point:** Marketing executive thrust into B2C lead buying
- **Journey:** Learning and implementing each discipline systematically
- **Endpoint:** Seasoned media buyer at the center of her organization's revenue engine

Sarah must appear in EVERY chapter as the vehicle for experiencing challenges and implementing solutions.

### Chapter Template (2,000 words)

Every chapter follows this structure:

```
OPENING VIGNETTE (400-500 words)
- Sarah encounters THIS chapter's specific challenge
- Show the problem through action and dialogue, not explanation
- Set up tension: what's at stake if she doesn't solve this?
- NO recap of previous chapters
- NO extended problem setup

CORE FRAMEWORK (800-1,000 words)
- The ONE system/approach that solves this problem
- Taught THROUGH Sarah implementing it (not explained to her)
- Concrete, specific, actionable
- Minimal bullets (only critical frameworks as memory devices)
- Flowing prose with strategic paragraph breaks

TACTICAL APPLICATION (400-500 words)
- 1-2 specific implementations (not 3-5)
- Show Sarah's results with concrete metrics
- Demonstrate the framework working in practice
- Brief, punchy, results-focused

TRANSITION (100-150 words)
- Bridge to next chapter's challenge
- NO summary of what was learned
- Set up next problem Sarah will face
- Forward momentum only
```

## Ruthless Elimination Rules

### ELIMINATE ENTIRELY

**B2B Content:**
- All B2B software/SaaS examples
- LinkedIn references and professional social selling
- Enterprise software pricing and sales cycles
- B2B-specific channels and tactics
- Keep ONLY: B2C consumer direct (mortgage, insurance, solar, home improvement)

**Redundancy Patterns:**
- Concept re-introductions (reader retains from first mention)
- "As we discussed in Chapter X" references
- "What you've learned so far" recaps
- Multiple examples proving the same point (keep strongest only)
- Extended problem setup before frameworks
- Nested sub-frameworks and multi-pillar breakdowns

**Structural Elements:**
- "Resources and tools" sections at chapter ends
- Footnote-style citations (move to chapter-end references only)
- More than 2 levels of subheadings
- "Common pitfalls" sections (integrate warnings into main narrative)
- Separate "Implementation" sections (fold into framework)
- Separate "Measuring success" sections (integrate metrics into results)

**Filler Language:**
- "Let's explore...", "Now we'll examine...", "It's important to understand..."
- "The key insight here is...", "What this really means is..."
- "As Sarah discovered...", "Sarah learned that..." (show, don't tell)
- Hyperbolic phrases like "...Changes Everything", "...That Changed Everything"
- Generic business-speak and jargon without specific meaning

### CONVERT TO TIGHT PROSE

**Bulleted Lists:**
- Convert 90%+ of bullets to flowing prose
- Keep bullets ONLY for:
  - Critical frameworks that serve as memory devices
  - Multi-step processes where order matters
  - Quick-reference checklists (use sparingly)
- Maximum 3-5 bullets per chapter (total)

**Examples and Case Studies:**
- One vivid example > three generic examples
- Show numbers and results, not lengthy backstory
- Integrate into Sarah's narrative, not separate case studies

**Statistics and Data:**
- One compelling data point > five similar statistics
- Integrate naturally into prose
- No parenthetical citations mid-sentence (move to footnotes)

## Quality Standards

### Every Sentence Must:
1. Advance Sarah's story, OR
2. Teach the core framework, OR
3. Provide tactical value

If a sentence doesn't do one of these three things, cut it.

### Prose Style Rules:

**DO:**
- Active voice, tight sentences
- Show through Sarah's experience
- Let data and results speak for themselves
- Write like talking to a sharp colleague
- Use specific numbers and concrete examples
- Maintain professional but accessible tone

**DON'T:**
- Use generic business-speak or jargon
- Editorialize or over-explain
- Use hyperbolic "...Everything" constructions (per user memory)
- Repeat concepts covered in previous chapters
- Assume readers need hand-holding
- Use nested bullets or complex formatting

### Pacing Checklist:

After editing each chapter, verify:
- [ ] 1,900-2,100 words total
- [ ] Sarah appears and drives the narrative
- [ ] One clear framework (not multiple pillars)
- [ ] 1-2 tactical applications (not 3-5)
- [ ] Minimal bullets (0-5 maximum)
- [ ] No concept redundancy with previous chapters
- [ ] No B2B content
- [ ] Tight prose with forward momentum
- [ ] 8-10 minute read time

## Examples: Before and After

### BEFORE (verbose, bullet-heavy):

"Sarah's first step was conducting a comprehensive analysis of their current contact practices. What she found was both typical and troubling.

**Current State Analysis:**
- Average time to first contact: 47 minutes
- Contact attempts per lead: 3.2 over 5 days
- Primary contact method: Phone calls during business hours
- Contact rate: 14% (industry average: 12-18%)
- Lead-to-opportunity conversion: 2.1%

'We're not actually bad at this,' Sarah realized. 'We're just average. And average isn't good enough when you're spending over a million dollars on leads.'"

**Word count:** 95 words

### AFTER (tight prose, action-focused):

"Sarah traced fifty leads through TechFlow's system. The pattern was damning: first contact averaged forty-seven minutes, reps made three half-hearted attempts over five days, and only fourteen percent ever answered the phone. They were spending a million dollars annually to reach people who'd moved on to competitors within the first hour."

**Word count:** 51 words (46% reduction)

---

### BEFORE (explaining, not showing):

"Through her research into consumer communication preferences, Sarah discovered that channel preferences were more nuanced than simple demographic categories suggested. Different consumers preferred different channels, and success depended on meeting them where they wanted to engage."

**Word count:** 40 words

### AFTER (showing through data):

"The data demolished her assumptions: twenty-five-year-olds responded to SMS at forty-seven percent but ignored email. Fifty-year-olds opened every email but blocked unknown callers. The playbook that worked for mortgage leads failed completely with solar prospects."

**Word count:** 38 words (5% reduction, but more vivid and specific)

---

### BEFORE (multi-pillar framework):

"Through her analysis of high-performing operations, Sarah identified five foundational elements that separated exceptional contact rates from average performance:

### Pillar 1: SMS-first speed systems
### Pillar 2: Consumer-controlled engagement
### Pillar 3: Timing and consumer respect
### Pillar 4: Personalization at scale
### Pillar 5: Quality measurement

Each pillar required specific implementation..."

**Word count:** 47 words + extensive expansion

### AFTER (single framework):

"The solution was simpler than Sarah expected: text first, text fast, and let prospects control what happens next. When TechFlow sent personalized SMS within two minutes—with a scheduling link, not a sales pitch—response rates tripled overnight."

**Word count:** 38 words (captures essence without pillar breakdown)

## Execution Protocol

### Edit Sequence:
1. Read entire chapter to understand Sarah's arc and core framework
2. Identify the ONE framework this chapter teaches
3. Identify 1-2 tactical applications that prove it works
4. Cut everything else ruthlessly
5. Rewrite remaining content as tight prose
6. Verify against quality checklist
7. Ensure smooth transition to next chapter

### Word Count Management:
- **Opening vignette:** 400-500 words (Sarah encounters problem)
- **Core framework:** 800-1,000 words (the solution)
- **Tactical application:** 400-500 words (results)
- **Transition:** 100-150 words (bridge to next chapter)
- **Total:** 1,900-2,100 words

### Red Flags to Watch:
- Chapter exceeds 2,100 words → cut more aggressively
- Sarah disappears for long stretches → reframe through her experience
- Multiple frameworks competing → pick the strongest one
- Bullets dominate → convert to prose
- Cross-chapter redundancy → trust reader's memory
- B2B examples → eliminate entirely

---

## Final Quality Gates

Before considering a chapter complete:

1. **Sarah Test:** Does Sarah drive this chapter's narrative from problem to solution?
2. **Framework Test:** Can you state the ONE core framework in a single sentence?
3. **Application Test:** Are the 1-2 tactical implementations specific and results-focused?
4. **Redundancy Test:** Is anything repeated from previous chapters?
5. **Bullet Test:** Are bullets minimal (0-5 per chapter)?
6. **Vertical Test:** Is all content B2C consumer direct focused?
7. **Prose Test:** Does it read like a book, not an outline?
8. **Word Test:** 1,900-2,100 words total?
9. **Pacing Test:** 8-10 minute engaging read?
10. **Momentum Test:** Does it transition smoothly to the next challenge?

## Appendix: Prohibited Phrases

Eliminate these patterns on sight:

- "Let's explore...", "Now we'll examine..."
- "The key insight here is...", "What this really means is..."
- "As Sarah discovered...", "Sarah learned that..."
- "It's important to understand that..."
- "This is where most teams...", "Many companies struggle with..."
- "The first thing you need to understand is..."
- "[Topic] isn't just about X—it's about Y"
- "...Changes Everything", "...That Changed Everything"
- "When done right...", "If implemented properly..."
- "The reality is that...", "The truth is that..."

Replace with direct, action-focused prose that shows rather than tells.

---

## Editor's Authority

When in doubt:
- **Cut more** (aim for 1,900 words rather than 2,100)
- **Choose action over explanation** (show Sarah doing, not learning)
- **Trust the reader** (they're smart, they retain, they don't need repetition)
- **Favor prose over bullets** (unless it's a critical memory device)
- **Eliminate B2B** (no exceptions)
- **Keep moving forward** (no looking back at previous chapters)

This specification is the contract between author and editor. When followed precisely, it will produce a tight, engaging, practical book that smart readers will devour and immediately apply.
